Behavior Methodology

Foundations of behavior assessment, behavior intervention planning, and evidence-based classroom practices.

Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA)

A Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) is a systematic process used to identify the function of a student’s challenging behavior and the environmental factors that influence it. One of the most commonly used tools in FBAs is the ABC (Antecedent–Behavior–Consequence) recording system.

An ABC FBA examines:

  • Antecedent: What happens right before the behavior?
  • Behavior: What exactly does the student do in observable terms?
  • Consequence: What occurs immediately after the behavior?

By analyzing patterns in ABC data, educators can determine whether the behavior serves a function such as:

  • Seeking attention
  • Escaping tasks or situations
  • Accessing preferred items or activities
  • Sensory stimulation

Understanding the function of behavior allows for the development of targeted and effective interventions.

Behavior Intervention Planning

After conducting an FBA, the next step is creating a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP). A strong BIP is proactive, skills-based, and rooted in positive behavior supports.

Key components of a quality BIP include:

  • Clear description of the target behavior
  • Identification of the function of the behavior
  • Specific proactive strategies
  • Replacement behaviors to be taught
  • Consequence strategies that reduce reinforcement of challenging behavior
  • Progress monitoring and data collection procedures

Effective BIPs emphasize teaching new skills rather than simply suppressing unwanted behavior.

Example Function-Based Strategy

Example: A student engages in disruptive behavior to escape difficult academic tasks.

A function-based intervention might include:

  • Providing task breaks after set intervals
  • Teaching the student to request help appropriately
  • Pre-teaching academic skills to build confidence
  • Reinforcing on-task behavior with praise or incentives
  • Adjusting work difficulty to match the student’s skill level

Evidence-Based Academic and Behavioral Practices

Many instructional practices that increase academic engagement also improve behavior. Evidence-based practices include:

  • Explicit instruction with modeling and guided practice
  • Active student responding (response cards, choral response, turn-and-talk)
  • Behavior-specific praise
  • Opportunities to respond at a high frequency
  • Self-monitoring tools
  • Positive reinforcement systems

When implemented consistently, these strategies improve both academic performance and classroom behavior.
